Since the Pokhran-II tests of 1998, India’s nuclear posture has been anchored by a steadfast commitment to a "No First Use" (NFU) policy. Predicated on the concept of credible minimum deterrence, the doctrine assured adversaries that Indian nuclear weapons were strictly political tools meant to deter nuclear blackmail, not instruments of warfighting.

However, the evolving security dynamics of the subcontinent are subjecting this doctrine to rigorous analytical scrutiny. The deployment of tactical nuclear weapons by regional adversaries, designed to lower the nuclear threshold and counter Indian conventional superiority, presents a complex strategic conundrum. How does a nation maintain the credibility of massive retaliation when faced with a localized, low-yield battlefield strike?

Simultaneously, the rapid modernization and expansion of nuclear arsenals across the Himalayas necessitate a continuous evaluation of what constitutes a "minimum" credible deterrent. As delivery systems become more sophisticated and survivable, the quantitative and qualitative requirements of the Indian arsenal must organically adapt to maintain strategic equilibrium.

While the official NFU doctrine remains the bedrock of Indian nuclear strategy, the discourse among strategic elites reveals a growing appetite for doctrinal flexibility. Ensuring that deterrence remains absolute requires not just hardware, but the communicated political will to employ it decisively should the red lines of sovereignty be breached.
